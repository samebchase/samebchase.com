The Industrial Tour

Most engineering students in Andhra Pradesh are made to go on an Industrial Tour. This is usually just an excuse to have fun traveling and spending time with classmates. It was decided that our class go on a tour of North India - in the winter. I was worried as to how cold it would be, as I had spoken to people who had gone earlier.

The tour started off with a train journey from Vijayawada to Delhi. As it was a Garib Rath train, the journey was pleasant. The train was eight hours late. I was shocked by how cold it was in Delhi. We were first taken to a hotel so that we could eat dinner prepared by the cooks who were employees of the tour company. After dinner, a nine hour bus ride to Shimla awaited us.

A few hours after reaching Shimla, we left for Kufri, a short drive from the hotel. To go to the top of the hill, we sat on groups of four horses connected by ropes which were led up the small hill by the handlers. A fall into the valley would have been rather painful, if not fatal.

It was here that I saw a Yak for the first time. Yaks are now near the top of the list of my favourite animals.

There many other tourists at Kufri; almost all of them students from Andhra Pradesh. People didn't do much other than drink tea and eat instant noodles. After many photos, it was decided that it was time to leave as it was getting dark.

Getting up the hill was the easy part. The temperature was dropping fast. A large number of people (> 200) were stuck without any horses to take them down. By then, my feet had begun to hurt. The others weren't doing great either. Several fainted. People were in pain, crying and generally panicking. Some of us decided to walk down as there was no other way to go. After about ten minutes in the cold, slippery snow, we found a group of horses and their handlers ready to take us down.

Riding down a two-way narrow snow covered path in the dark was scary. After fifteen nervous minutes of trusting our lives to the horses and their handlers, we finally reached the bottom. We were so relieved to have survived what initially seemed a harmless few hours playing in the snow.

Another long bus journey later, we arrived in Manali. After sleeping in the warm, wooden hotel, we proceeded to the Solang Valley. It was just like the classic mental image that I had of snowy mountains. Snow-covered hills were all around, and snow mixed with horse dung underfoot.

The entire area was packed with students from Andhra Pradesh. There was simply no free space. People were learning how to ski, Paragliding, Zorbing, riding ATVs, eating instant noodles and taking photos for their online albums all at the same time. Chickening out of the adventure sports, I didn't do much other than go on the Cable Car ride up a mountain which no one else wanted to go on.

Here we were all given thick, warm coats (or ski suits) and rubber boots. It was all fine for a few hours, until my feet started hurting again. One of the guys from our SUV (we split into 'skiing' groups) still was waiting for his turn to paraglide. Meanwhile, everyone else was desperate to go, as our feet were hurting. After what seemed like ages, our guy landed safely and we drove back to the hotel.

My feet were numb by then; the right one worse than the left. It looked like there were two different types of skin on my right sole. One pale white and cold patch covering more than half the sole and normal, pink warmer flesh. After a quick call home and reading the Wikipedia article on Frostbite I started gently re-warming my feet in front of the room heater (~ 70 cm away). As my feet slowly started coming back to life, I also decided to do the 42 degrees (Celsius) water bath as suggested in the article, which felt quite nice.

I had totally neglected protecting my feet on the trip. Sneakers, rubber boots and a double sock layer are all inadequate for spending time in the snow. I don't know how everyone else managed, but I dread going back into the snow wearing any of these.

The next day we got onto the bus to Chandigarh. We didn't do much there apart from a brief visit to the Rock Garden. We had a short Industrial visit and then proceeded to Delhi.

Agra was next on the packed itinerary. We left Delhi late and arrived in Agra in the evening. The gates close at 5. Our group traveled in two buses: one for the girls and some of their boyfriends, and the other was boys-only. The girls' bus reached just in time, but the second bus was not so lucky. After pleading unsuccessfully with the guards (tickets were pre-bought) at two different gates, four of us went to the banks of the Yamuna and saw the Taj Mahal from behind. While it was quite close by, the upward angle at which we had to look was not nearly as good as what the people who had gone inside would have seen.

One of the interesting things we did on the tour was an appointment with Mr. Ram Jethmalani all thanks to our Class Rep (CR, for short). I hadn't heard of him before the trip, but his very impressive Wikipedia article, his obvious fame and importance made me feel honoured and privileged meet him. We asked him a few questions about various things and his answers were enlightening and interesting.

He spoke for a while and went on several tangents, which we were quite happy he did, as the lesser we spoke, the better. He was nice enough to give each one us all a fruit drink and a box containing a generous amount of snacks.

As our hotel was close to Karol Bagh, the evening was spent shopping. I couldn't think of anything to buy as it was primarily clothes that were available there. It was that evening that I finally got the Delhi-belly I had been dreading for the past two years. Luckily, we were to leave for Vijayawada the next evening. By this time, I was sick of the cold weather and was yearning for the pleasant South Indian winter.

The tour had it all: there was fun, enjoyment, togetherness with danger, sicknesses, tantrums and fights mixed in which brought out the best, and worst in us. New friendships were formed and old ones tested. It truly was a once in a lifetime experience that it had been hyped up to be.
